# Courier and Driver Module

## Intake additions

Capture:
- vehicle year, make, model, cargo capacity, condition, title status, and commercial-use restrictions
- driver's license class, driving record, age restrictions, and background-check readiness
- insurance status and ability to obtain commercial coverage
- DOT, MC, CDL, TSA, TWIC, HIPAA, bloodborne-pathogen, OSHA, or hazmat requirements where relevant
- preferred schedule, route radius, lifting capacity, and willingness to cross state lines
- smartphone, navigation, proof-of-delivery, and dispatch capability

## Opportunity ladder

Rank in this order unless territory evidence supports another sequence:

1. Vehicle-compatible gig or subcontract work for immediate proof of performance.
2. Scheduled local routes with repeat volume.
3. Specialized delivery such as medical, pharmacy, lab, legal, auto parts, or B2B supplies.
4. Direct local commercial accounts.
5. Multi-driver routes or fleet contracts.
6. Dispatch, brokerage, or managed delivery operations where legally appropriate.

## Vehicle pathways

- Bicycle/e-bike: dense urban food, documents, pharmacy, and local parcels.
- Sedan: documents, pharmacy, specimens where compliant, small parcels, and B2B parts.
- SUV/minivan: larger parcel mix, multi-stop routes, medical supplies, and retail overflow.
- Cargo/Sprinter van: bulky freight, routed B2B, final mile, equipment, and expedited work.
- Pickup/flatbed: construction materials, equipment, landscaping, and specialized local hauling.
- Box truck: final-mile furniture/appliance, palletized freight, commercial routes, and contracted distribution.

Do not assume a vehicle qualifies. Verify current requirements for each company or contract.

## Skills to acquire

1. Safe handling and proof of delivery.
2. Route planning and time-window management.
3. Commercial insurance literacy.
4. Cost-per-mile and cost-per-stop calculation.
5. Quoting and minimum-charge discipline.
6. Direct-account prospecting.
7. Service-level agreement management.
8. Driver recruiting and onboarding.
9. Dispatch and exception management.
10. Fleet utilization and maintenance planning.

## Courier unit economics

Calculate at minimum:
- revenue per route, stop, mile, and hour
- loaded versus deadhead miles
- fuel and charging cost
- maintenance and depreciation reserve
- insurance allocation
- tolls, parking, supplies, and platform fees
- driver or subcontractor cost
- gross margin and contribution margin

Reject opportunities that generate activity without acceptable contribution margin.

## Direct-client targets

Potential account categories:
- pharmacies
- laboratories
- clinics and hospitals
- law firms and title companies
- auto-parts distributors
- print shops
- wholesalers
- manufacturers
- florists and caterers
- local retailers
- property-management companies
- government agencies and educational institutions

## CEO transition markers

- Operator: completes deliveries reliably.
- Contractor: controls pricing, records, insurance, and client communication.
- Owner: has repeat routes, SOPs, and at least one backup driver.
- CEO: manages dispatch, sales, client retention, finances, and multiple operators without driving every route.
